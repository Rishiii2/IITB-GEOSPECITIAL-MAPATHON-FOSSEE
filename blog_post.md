# Mapping the Future: How I Used AI to Find India's Best Renewable Energy Zones
## A Personal Walkthrough of My IIT Bombay FOSSEE Mapathon Project

Ever since I learned about India's massive goal to hit five hundred gigawatts of renewable energy capacity by the end of the decade, I've been fascinated by the sheer scale of the challenge. The tricky part isn't just building the solar panels or wind turbines; it is figuring out exactly where to put them. You can't just build a solar farm in the middle of nowhere and expect it to be cheap to connect to the national grid, and you definitely can't build a wind farm on a steep mountain slope. Most of the time, urban planners use something called Multi Criteria Decision Analysis to figure this out, which basically means humans sit down and guess how important each factor is. For the IIT Bombay FOSSEE Mapathon, I really wanted to try something different. I wanted to see if I could use Machine Learning to completely remove human bias and let the data speak for itself.

My initial idea was super simple, maybe a bit too simple. I figured I could just download some open-source map data, stack it on top of some weather data, and run a quick script to find the best spots. But as soon as I started downloading the datasets, I realized this project was going to be way more intense than I originally thought. The FOSSEE guidelines encourage using open-source tools, so I committed to doing the entire project using Python, QGIS, and open data platforms like NASA and OpenStreetMap. The goal was to build a spatial matrix that covered the entire Indian subcontinent and analyze it pixel by pixel.

The very first massive roadblock I hit was a literal computer crash. I downloaded the raw OpenStreetMap database for India, which was a compressed binary file taking up over one point six gigabytes of space. I wrote a standard Python script to open it and extract the national electrical grid and the locations of existing solar farms. Almost instantly, my computer's RAM maxed out and the script threw a massive MemoryError. I spent a whole day reading through documentation and finally discovered the GDAL library and a package called pyogrio. By using these tools, I was able to bypass Python's normal memory limits and stream the data at the C++ level directly from my hard drive. It felt amazing to finally watch the script successfully extract over forty five thousand power lines without crashing my laptop.

After fixing the memory issue, I had to deal with the climate data. I needed thirty years of historical weather data for solar irradiance and wind speed, which I decided to pull from the NASA Prediction Of Worldwide Energy Resources API. The problem was that the Indian subcontinent is huge, and NASA's servers kept rejecting my requests because they were too large. To get around this, I had to write a script that sliced a map of India into smaller ten by ten degree bounding boxes. My code essentially crawled across the map, downloading the weather data piece by piece, and then I used the xarray library to mathematically stitch all those pieces back together into one giant, seamless grid. 

Once I had all the data, it was time to build the actual model. I gathered five key pieces of information for every single pixel in the country: the solar potential, the wind potential, the elevation, the terrain slope, and the exact distance to the nearest power line. Instead of telling the computer which of these factors was the most important, I fed the AI the exact GPS coordinates of over two thousand active, highly successful renewable energy farms that I found in the OpenStreetMap data. I told the XGBoost algorithm to look at the environment underneath those specific farms and learn the pattern. The algorithm basically builds a bunch of decision trees and keeps correcting its own mistakes by minimizing its loss function until it figures out the exact mathematical signature of a successful site. 

When the model finished training, I was honestly shocked by the results. It hit an accuracy of over eighty seven percent. To make sure the model wasn't just guessing blindly, I integrated a tool called SHAP, which uses game theory to calculate exactly how much each feature contributed to the AI's final decision. This meant I could look at any spot on the map and mathematically prove exactly why the AI thought it was a good or bad location for a solar farm. Finally, I exported the best zones into a spatial file and wrote a visualization script using matplotlib to render the final cartographic maps. It was an incredibly challenging project that pushed my coding skills to the limit, but seeing the final glowing heatmap of India's renewable future made every single crashed script totally worth it.
